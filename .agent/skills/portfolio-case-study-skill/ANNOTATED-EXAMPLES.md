## Example 1: Strong Hero Section

### From: UWGLA (Josie Grimmett)

```
# 01 United Way of Greater Los Angeles

Website Design | Branding | Interaction Design | Animation | 
Social Design | Editorial Design

## Supporting solutions to inequality, advancing economic mobility, 
   and uplifting underserved communities
```

**Why this works:**

‚úÖ **Project number creates portfolio structure** - "01" suggests a curated collection  
‚úÖ **Discipline tags are specific** - Not just "design" but 6 specific areas  
‚úÖ **Value proposition is human-focused** - Talks about impact, not features  
‚úÖ **Tone matches client** - Empowering language for nonprofit work  
‚úÖ **Immediately clear what the project is** - Rebrand for a nonprofit

**Apply this pattern:**
```

[Specific Discipline 1] | [Specific Discipline 2] | [Specific Discipline 3]

## [One sentence about the impact/value, not the deliverable]
```

## Example 3: Excellent Research Framing

### From: Knowlex (Florinda Sgueglia)

```
### Research Goals

We want to investigate how people relate to EDtech platforms, 
so that we understand how users can swap different kinds of 
knowledge through a tech product.
```

**Why this works:**

‚úÖ **Formula: "investigate X so that understand Y"** - Clear cause and effect  
‚úÖ **Specific to the product** - Not generic "understand users"  
‚úÖ **Implies action** - Understanding will lead to design decisions  
‚úÖ **Accessible language** - No jargon

**Compare to weaker versions:**

‚ùå "Research goal: Understand users"  
‚ùå "We wanted to learn more about our target audience"  
‚ùå "Conduct user research to inform design decisions"

**Apply this pattern:**
```
We want to [investigate/understand/explore] [specific aspect], 
so that [we can achieve/design/solve] [specific outcome].
```

## Example 5: Design Decision Explanation

### From: UWGLA (Josie Grimmett)

```
Typography sets the tone in this ecosystem, flexing between 
bold or refined, energetic or professional, gutsy or wise. 
We enhanced UWGLA's original brand colors with deeper, more 
vibrant hues, grounded in the energy of Los Angeles.
```

**Why this works:**

‚úÖ **Explains the "why"** - Typography is flexible because the brand needs to flex  
‚úÖ **Specific contrasts** - "bold or refined", "energetic or professional"  
‚úÖ **Grounds decision in context** - "energy of Los Angeles"  
‚úÖ **Builds on existing** - "enhanced UWGLA's original brand colors"  
‚úÖ **Poetic but precise** - Evocative language that still communicates clearly

**Compare to weaker versions:**

‚ùå "We chose typography that works for the brand"  
‚ùå "Colors were selected to be vibrant and energetic"  
‚ùå "The typeface reflects the client's values"

**Apply this pattern:**
```
[Design element] [what it does] by [specific qualities/contrasts]. 
We [action taken] [rationale grounded in project context].
```

## Example 8: Weak ‚Üí Strong Comparison

### Weak Version:
```
I designed a new app that helps people learn from each other. 
It has a clean, modern interface with an easy-to-use design. 
Users really liked it.
```

**Problems:**
‚ùå Vague ("new app", "helps people")  
‚ùå Buzzwords ("clean", "modern", "easy-to-use")  
‚ùå No specifics ("users really liked it")  
‚ùå No process shown  
‚ùå No challenges mentioned

### Strong Version:
```
Knowlex is a knowledge-exchange platform where users teach skills 
they know to earn credits for learning new skills. After interviewing 
5 users (2 teachers, 2 students, 1 skeptic), I identified that community 
and flexibility were more important than breadth of courses. 

I designed a credit system and two-sided marketplace in Figma, tested 
with 4 users, and iterated based on feedback that the checkout flow 
was confusing. The final prototype increased task completion from 
60% to 85%.
```

**Improvements:**
‚úÖ Specific concept  
‚úÖ Actual research numbers  
‚úÖ Concrete findings  
‚úÖ Named tools  
‚úÖ Iteration based on data  
‚úÖ Quantified outcome

## Red Flags to Avoid

### üö© Red Flag 1: Feature List Without Context
```
‚ùå "The app includes: user profiles, messaging, search, and filters"
‚úÖ "Based on interviews with 5 users who struggled to find relevant 
   content, I prioritized advanced filtering (by topic, level, format)

### üö© Red Flag 2: Process Without Decision
```
‚ùå "I created wireframes and then moved to high-fidelity mockups"

   wireframes to validate the information hierarchy before investing 
   time in visual design"
```

```
‚ùå "I learned the importance of user research and iteration"

   what I thought was clear ('Resources') confused 4/5 users, leading

## Quick Reference: Sentence Starters

### For Introducing Projects:
- "[Client] is a [type] that [does what]. They needed..."
- "[Number] of [users] struggle with [problem]. I designed..."
- "How do you [achieve goal] when [constraint exists]?"

### For Explaining Research:
- "We wanted to investigate [X] so that we could understand [Y]"
- "After speaking with [number] [users], I learned..."
- "[Number] out of [number] users mentioned..."

### For Design Decisions:
- "I chose [X] because [reasoning based on research/constraints]"
- "[Element] needed to [goal], so I [decision]"
- "Based on [evidence], I prioritized [X] over [Y]"

### For Iterations:
- "Testing revealed [problem], so I [solution]"
- "Users struggled with [X], leading me to [change]"
- "After [feedback], I improved [element] by [specific change]"

### For Outcomes:
- "[Metric] improved from [before] to [after]"
- "Post-launch, [number]% of users [achievement]"
- "The client reported [specific feedback or result]"

### For Reflection:
- "I learned to [specific skill/approach] after [experience]"
- "If I could do this again, I would [specific change] because [reasoning]"
- "This project taught me [specific lesson with example]"
